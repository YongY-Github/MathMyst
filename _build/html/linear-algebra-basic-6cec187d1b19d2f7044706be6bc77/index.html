2. Linear Algebra

Matrix Basics¶

Matrix addition and subtraction: If A=(aij)\mathbf{A} = (a_{ij})A=(aij​) and B=(bij)\mathbf{B} = (b_{ij})B=(bij​) are two m×nm \times nm×n matrices of same dimension, then A+B\mathbf{A} + \mathbf{B}A+B is defined as (aij+bij)(a_{ij} + b_{ij})(aij​+bij​). That is, we add element by element the two matrices.
Clearly A+B=B+A\mathbf{A} + \mathbf{B} = \mathbf{B} + \mathbf{A}A+B=B+A. The rule applies to matrix subtraction.

Transpose of a matrix: If A\mathbf{A}A is an m×nm \times nm×n matrix, then A′\mathbf{A}'A′ is the n×mn \times mn×m matrix whose rows are the columns of A\mathbf{A}A. So A′=(aji)\mathbf{A}' = (a_{ji})A′=(aji​). For example:

[abcd]′=[acbd]\begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix}' = \begin{bmatrix} a & c \\ b & d \end{bmatrix}[ac​bd​]′=[ab​cd​]
(1)

Note: (A+B)′=A′+B′(\mathbf{A} + \mathbf{B})' = \mathbf{A}' + \mathbf{B}'(A+B)′=A′+B′, however (AB)′=B′A′(\mathbf{A}\mathbf{B})' = \mathbf{B}'\mathbf{A}'(AB)′=B′A′.

Null matrix: Has all elements as 0. Clearly A+0m,n=0m,n+A=A\mathbf{A} + \mathbf{0}_{m,n} = \mathbf{0}_{m,n} + \mathbf{A} = \mathbf{A}A+0m,n​=0m,n​+A=A for all m×nm \times nm×n matrices.

Scalar multiplication: If A=(aij)\mathbf{A} = (a_{ij})A=(aij​) then for any constant kkk, define kA=(kaij)k\mathbf{A} = (k a_{ij})kA=(kaij​). That is, multiply each element in A\mathbf{A}A by kkk.

Matrix multiplication: Say A\mathbf{A}A is m×nm \times nm×n, and B\mathbf{B}B is n×pn \times pn×p, then the m×pm \times pm×p matrix AB\mathbf{A}\mathbf{B}AB is the product of A\mathbf{A}A and B\mathbf{B}B. For example:

[abcd][efgh]=[ae+bgaf+bhce+dgcf+dh]\begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} e & f \\ g & h \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} ae + bg & af + bh \\ ce + dg & cf + dh \end{bmatrix}[ac​bd​][eg​fh​]=[ae+bgce+dg​af+bhcf+dh​]
(2)

and the matrices A\mathbf{A}A and B\mathbf{B}B are conformable.

Diagonal matrix: A square n×nn \times nn×n matrix A\mathbf{A}A is diagonal if all entries off the ‘main diagonal’ are zero, i.e.

A=[a110⋯00a22⋯0⋮⋮⋱⋮00⋯ann]\mathbf{A} = \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & a_{22} & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & a_{nn} \end{bmatrix}A=⎣⎡​a11​0⋮0​0a22​⋮0​⋯⋯⋱⋯​00⋮ann​​⎦⎤​
(3)

Note: The elements in the diagonal of A\mathbf{A}A are the same as those in A′\mathbf{A}'A′.

Trace of a square matrix: If A\mathbf{A}A is a square matrix, the trace of A\mathbf{A}A, denoted tr⁡(A)\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{A})tr(A), is the sum of the elements on the main/leading diagonal of A\mathbf{A}A.

Identity matrix: Denoted by In\mathbf{I}_nIn​, the n×nn \times nn×n diagonal matrix with aii=1a_{ii} = 1aii​=1 for all iii. That is:

In=[10⋯001⋯0⋮⋮⋱⋮00⋯1]\mathbf{I}_n = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & 1 \end{bmatrix}In​=⎣⎡​10⋮0​01⋮0​⋯⋯⋱⋯​00⋮1​⎦⎤​
(4)

Symmetric matrix: A square matrix A\mathbf{A}A is symmetric if A=A′\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{A}'A=A′. The identity matrix and the square null matrix are symmetric.

Idempotent matrix¶

A square matrix is said to be idempotent if A2=A\mathbf{A}^2 = \mathbf{A}A2=A. Below is an example (try squaring the matrix and see what you get).

[1000]\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}[10​00​]
(5)

Singular Matrix: A matrix that is linearly dependent (in the columns or rows) is singular and has determinant of zero. Note that any two (or three) vectors x\mathbf{x}x and y\mathbf{y}y (and z\mathbf{z}z) are said to be linearly dependent iff one can be written as a scalar multiple of the other. Two vectors x≠0\mathbf{x} \neq \mathbf{0}x=0 and y≠0\mathbf{y} \neq \mathbf{0}y=0 are said to be linearly independent iff the ONLY solution to ax+by=0a\mathbf{x} + b\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{0}ax+by=0 is a=0a = 0a=0 AND b=0b = 0b=0. And x\mathbf{x}x and y\mathbf{y}y are said to be orthogonal.

Some Notes on Matrices¶

  1. Usually AB≠BA\mathbf{A}\mathbf{B} \neq \mathbf{B}\mathbf{A}AB=BA. For example, try A=[2031]\mathbf{A} = \begin{bmatrix}2 & 0 \\ 3 & 1\end{bmatrix}A=[23​01​] and B=[0100]\mathbf{B} = \begin{bmatrix}0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0\end{bmatrix}B=[00​10​].

  2. We can have AB=0\mathbf{A}\mathbf{B} = \mathbf{0}AB=0 even if A\mathbf{A}A or B\mathbf{B}B are not zero. For example, try A=[6−12−36]\mathbf{A} = \begin{bmatrix}6 & -12 \\ -3 & 6\end{bmatrix}A=[6−3​−126​] and B=[12663]\mathbf{B} = \begin{bmatrix}12 & 6 \\ 6 & 3\end{bmatrix}B=[126​63​].

  3. Say, A=[4812]\mathbf{A} = \begin{bmatrix}4 & 8 \\ 1 & 2\end{bmatrix}A=[41​82​], B=[2122]\mathbf{B} = \begin{bmatrix}2 & 1 \\ 2 & 2\end{bmatrix}B=[22​12​], and C=[−2142]\mathbf{C} = \begin{bmatrix}-2 & 1 \\ 4 & 2\end{bmatrix}C=[−24​12​]. We find that AB=AC\mathbf{A}\mathbf{B} = \mathbf{A}\mathbf{C}AB=AC even though B≠C\mathbf{B} \neq \mathbf{C}B=C.

  4. Say A\mathbf{A}A and B\mathbf{B}B are singular matrices. Then AB\mathbf{A}\mathbf{B}AB will not be zero, although the product will be a singular matrix.
